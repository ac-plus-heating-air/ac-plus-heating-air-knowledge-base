### What commercial HVAC is and how it differs from residential HVAC

Commercial HVAC is the category of mechanical systems designed to heat, cool, ventilate, and control air quality in non-residential buildings. The distinction from [residential HVAC](https://ac-plus-heating-air.mintlify.site/residential-hvac) goes beyond size alone. Commercial systems use fundamentally different equipment configurations, control architectures, and load calculations. A residential system typically serves one household with a single thermostat zone; a commercial system often serves dozens of zones across multiple floors, with equipment staged to match occupancy schedules and variable internal loads from lighting, equipment, and people. Rooftop units (RTUs) are the most common commercial platform, mounted above the conditioned space and connected to a commercial ductwork network below. Larger facilities integrate a building automation system (BAS) that monitors and adjusts every zone automatically.

### Measurable attributes: capacity, coverage area, and energy efficiency rating

Commercial HVAC systems are specified by three primary measurable attributes: system capacity in tons, coverage area in square feet, and energy efficiency rating expressed as EER, SEER2, or IEER. System capacity starts at 5 tons for small retail or office suites and scales past 1,000 tons for large industrial or campus facilities. Coverage area follows a similar range, from roughly 1,000 square feet for a small storefront to 500,000 square feet or more for a distribution center or hospital wing. Energy efficiency rating determines operating cost over the life of the equipment. IEER (Integrated Energy Efficiency Ratio) is the standard metric for commercial unitary equipment because it accounts for part-load performance, which is where most commercial systems spend the majority of their runtime. Selecting the right capacity for the actual load is critical; an oversized unit short-cycles and fails prematurely, while an undersized one runs continuously without reaching setpoint.

### Key components of a commercial HVAC system

Commercial Mechanical Systems are made up of several interdependent components, each with a defined role in conditioning and distributing air. The components below are the core parts found in most commercial installations.

- **Rooftop units (RTUs):** Rooftop units house the compressor, condenser, evaporator coil, and blower in a single weatherproof cabinet mounted on the roof, keeping mechanical equipment off the occupied floor.

- **Commercial ductwork network:** The ductwork network distributes conditioned air from the RTU to supply registers throughout the building and returns it to the unit for reconditioning.

- **Building automation system (BAS):** A BAS connects thermostats, sensors, dampers, and equipment controllers into one platform, allowing facility managers to schedule setpoints, monitor faults, and track energy use from a single interface.

- **Variable air volume (VAV) boxes:** VAV boxes modulate airflow to individual zones based on demand, reducing energy consumption compared to constant-volume systems.

- **Economizers:** Economizers use outdoor air for free cooling when outside conditions allow, cutting compressor runtime during mild weather. A meaningful efficiency gain in a high-desert climate with cool nights.

### Common problems and signs a commercial HVAC system needs service

Commercial HVAC problems tend to surface as comfort complaints, unusual energy bills, or equipment faults logged by the BAS. Uneven temperatures across zones are one of the earliest signs. Often caused by a failing VAV box, a clogged filter bank, or a refrigerant charge that has drifted out of spec. RTUs in desert climates accumulate dust and debris on condenser coils faster than units in milder regions, which raises head pressure and forces the compressor to work harder. Replacement becomes necessary when repair costs approach or exceed the value of continued operation, typically when a unit is past 15 to 20 years of age and experiencing repeated compressor or heat exchanger failures. Refrigerant work on commercial equipment requires EPA 608-certified handling by a licensed technician, so any leak diagnosis or recharge must be performed by a qualified contractor. Ignoring early warning signs — short cycling, ice on the evaporator coil, or a BAS fault code that keeps returning — shortens equipment life and raises the commercial HVAC installation cost of the eventual replacement by accelerating structural damage to the unit. ### Where commercial HVAC applies and what a maintenance contract covers

Commercial Heating and Cooling applies to any occupied non-residential structure: office buildings, retail centers, restaurants, medical clinics, schools, warehouses, and light industrial facilities. Each building type has a different load profile. A restaurant kitchen generates far more heat per square foot than an open-plan office, and a medical clinic requires tighter humidity control than a retail store. A commercial HVAC maintenance contract formalizes the inspection and service schedule that keeps these systems operating within their design parameters. A standard contract covers filter replacement, coil cleaning, belt and bearing inspection, refrigerant level verification, electrical connection tightening, and BAS fault review on a scheduled basis. Typically quarterly for high-use facilities and semi-annually for lighter-use buildings. Preventive maintenance under a contract reduces unplanned downtime and extends equipment service life, which directly affects the total commercial HVAC installation cost when replacement eventually becomes necessary.
