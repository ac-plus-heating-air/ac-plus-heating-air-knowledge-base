### What the Inland Empire is

The Inland Empire is a bi-county metropolitan region in Southern California, formally identified as the Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario metro area by federal geographic standards. It sits east of Los Angeles County and north of San Diego County, separated from the coast by the San Gabriel and San Bernardino mountain ranges. Those mountains block marine air, which is why the IE runs hotter and drier than the coastal zones that border it. Elevation across the region ranges from roughly 800 feet in the lower valleys to over 11,500 feet at the highest peaks, creating distinct climate bands within a single service area. ### Common HVAC problems in the Inland Empire

Inland Empire air conditioning repair calls follow predictable patterns tied to the regional climate. The most frequent issues are refrigerant loss from thermally stressed line sets, compressor overload from sustained high-ambient operation, and clogged condenser coils from blowing dust and debris. High desert communities above 3,500 feet add freeze-up risk in winter for heat pump systems not rated for low-ambient operation. Duct systems in older IE homes often lack adequate insulation for attic temperatures that exceed 140°F in July, causing supply air to gain heat before it reaches the living space. The problems below appear most often across the region.

- **Refrigerant loss:** Refrigerant loss accelerates when line sets expand and contract repeatedly through 90-degree daily temperature swings.

- **Compressor overload:** Compressor overload occurs when ambient temperatures stay above 100°F and the condenser cannot reject heat fast enough.

- **Clogged condenser coils:** Clogged condenser coils result from the fine dust and particulate common to desert and high desert air.

- **Undersized duct insulation:** Undersized duct insulation allows attic heat to raise supply air temperature before it reaches the registers.

- **Heat pump freeze-up:** Heat pump freeze-up affects units in High Desert communities when overnight lows drop below the system's rated low-ambient threshold.
