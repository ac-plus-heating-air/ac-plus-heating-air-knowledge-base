What an air handler is and how it works

An air handler is the indoor half of a split HVAC system. It is responsible for moving air across the evaporator coil and distributing conditioned air through supply ducts. Three core components make up the unit: a blower motor, an evaporator coil, and an air filter. The blower motor pulls return air from the living space. The evaporator coil then absorbs heat from that air, and the filter traps particulates before they reach the coil or the duct system.

The AHU does not generate cooling on its own. Refrigerant lines connect it to the outdoor condenser unit, which removes the heat the evaporator coil absorbed. Without that outdoor unit, the air handler moves air but cannot cool it.

Measurable attributes of an air handler

Air handler performance comes down to three measurable attributes: airflow capacity, static pressure rating, and filter efficiency. Each one has a specific range that determines whether the unit is right for a given space.

  • Airflow capacity: Airflow capacity is measured in cubic feet per minute (CFM). Residential air handlers typically range from 400 to 2,000 CFM, with the correct value determined by the square footage and load calculation for the home.
  • Static pressure rating: Static pressure rating measures the resistance the blower motor overcomes to push air through the duct system, expressed in inches of water column (in. WC). Most residential units operate between 0.1 and 2.0 in. WC; a reading above the rated maximum signals a restriction such as a clogged filter or undersized ductwork.
  • Filter efficiency: Filter efficiency is rated on the MERV scale from 1 to 16. Higher MERV ratings capture smaller particles but increase static pressure, so the filter must match what the blower motor is rated to handle.

Matching all three attributes to the home's load is what air handler installation requires before any equipment is ordered. Oversizing the blower or undersizing the filter slot creates airflow problems that no amount of refrigerant adjustment will fix.

Common problems and signs of a failing air handler

Air handler problems fall into three categories: mechanical failures, airflow restrictions, and refrigerant-side issues. Recognizing the signs early prevents a partial failure from becoming a full replacement.

  • Air handler not blowing air: An air handler not blowing air points to a failed blower motor, a tripped capacitor, or a broken belt on older belt-drive units. The blower motor is the most common single-point failure in an AHU.
  • Frozen evaporator coil: A frozen coil forms when airflow drops below the minimum CFM the coil needs to absorb heat, usually from a clogged filter or a closed supply register. Ice on the coil blocks airflow further and can damage the compressor in the outdoor unit.
  • Excessive static pressure: Static pressure above the rated maximum strains the blower motor and reduces airflow to every room on the system. A dirty filter, collapsed flex duct, or an undersized return grille are the most common causes.
  • Water leaks around the unit: Condensate drains from the evaporator coil through a drain pan and a drain line. A clogged drain line backs up into the pan and overflows, which causes water damage to the surrounding structure and triggers float switches that shut the system down.
  • Unusual noise: Rattling points to loose panels or debris in the blower wheel. Squealing points to a worn blower motor bearing. Both need attention before the component fails completely.

A static pressure test during a maintenance visit catches most of these problems before they produce a symptom the homeowner notices.

Air handler vs. Condensing unit: understanding the contrast

An air handler and a condensing unit are opposite halves of a split system. Confusing the two leads to misdiagnosed repairs. The air handling unit sits indoors and moves air; the condensing unit sits outdoors and rejects heat. The AHU contains the evaporator coil, which absorbs heat from indoor air. The condensing unit contains the compressor and condenser coil, which release that heat outside.

A failure in one unit does not always mean both need replacement. A failed blower motor is an air handler repair. A failed compressor is a condensing unit repair. Matching the replacement component to the existing unit's capacity matters in either case, because a new high-efficiency air handling unit paired with an old low-efficiency condenser runs below the rated SEER of either unit alone.

Where air handlers apply in residential and light commercial settings

Air handlers serve any building that uses a ducted split system for heating or cooling. One unit. In residential settings, a single AHU typically conditions the entire home from one central location. Larger homes sometimes use two units zoned to separate floors or wings, each with its own thermostat and blower sized to that zone's CFM requirement.

Light commercial buildings use the same equipment category, though the units are larger and the static pressure ratings are higher to push air through longer duct runs. MERV rating requirements differ: commercial settings often specify MERV 13 filters to meet indoor air quality standards, while most residential systems run MERV 8 to MERV 11 without exceeding the blower motor's rated static pressure.
