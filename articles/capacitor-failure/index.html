What is capacitor failure

Capacitor failure is the inability of a run or start capacitor to store and release electrical charge within its rated specifications. It is a type of HVAC Electrical Failure, placing it in the same category as contactor faults, wiring shorts, and motor winding failures. When a capacitor can no longer hold its charge, the motor it serves. Compressor, condenser fan, or blower. Loses the electrical boost it needs to start or run. The result is an AC system that struggles to turn on, runs poorly, or shuts down entirely.

A capacitor fault is not a gradual performance issue you tune out over time. Left unaddressed, it forces the motor to draw excess current on every start attempt, which shortens motor life and can escalate to compressor failure. A far more serious and expensive repair.

Measurable attributes of a capacitor fault

Capacitor failure has three measurable attributes that a technician checks during AC capacitor failure diagnosis: capacitance deviation, operating voltage rating, and failure mode type.

  • Capacitance deviation: Capacitance deviation measures how far the stored charge has drifted from the rated value. Industry tolerance is ±6% of the rated microfarad (µF) value. A reading outside that band confirms the component has failed, even if the unit has not yet stopped running.
  • Operating voltage rating: Operating voltage rating is the maximum AC voltage the capacitor is designed to handle continuously. Most residential units use capacitors rated at 370V or 440V AC. Installing a replacement with a lower voltage rating than the original causes premature failure and voids the repair.
  • Failure mode type: Failure mode type describes how the capacitor failed. The three modes are open-circuit (no continuity, motor will not start), short-circuit (capacitor draws full current and trips the breaker), and reduced capacitance (the unit starts but runs weakly, often causing short cycling as the system overheats and cuts out).

A technician uses a multimeter or dedicated capacitor tester to measure µF directly. Visual inspection alone is not reliable. A capacitor can read out of spec with no visible bulging or leaking.

Capacitor failure symptoms in a Victorville home

Capacitor failure symptoms follow a recognizable pattern that homeowners in Victorville notice before the system quits entirely. The Mojave Desert climate pushes capacitors hard: sustained outdoor temperatures above 100°F accelerate dielectric degradation inside the aluminum casing, shortening the component's service life compared to milder climates.

  • Humming with no startup: Humming with no startup happens when the compressor or fan motor receives power but the capacitor cannot deliver the start torque. The motor hums for a few seconds, then the thermal overload trips.
  • Slow fan spin: Slow fan spin on the condenser unit signals a weakened run capacitor. The fan turns, but airflow across the coil drops, raising head pressure and reducing cooling output.
  • AC not cooling: Capacitor failure causing AC not cooling is the most common complaint. The compressor may start intermittently or not at all, so the refrigerant circuit never reaches operating pressure.
  • Frequent shutdowns: Frequent shutdowns occur when reduced capacitance lets the motor overheat on every cycle. The system trips its internal protection, rests, restarts briefly, and shuts down again. A pattern that appears with a frozen evaporator coil, so a technician must distinguish between the two causes.
  • Higher energy use: Higher energy use results from a motor running outside its design parameters. A degraded capacitor forces the motor to draw more current to produce the same output.

How capacitor failure relates to the broader AC system

Capacitor failure sits at the intersection of the electrical and mechanical subsystems of a split-system air conditioner. The run capacitor keeps the compressor motor and condenser fan motor running in phase; the start capacitor gives the compressor the extra torque to overcome static refrigerant pressure at startup. When either fails, the mechanical load on the motor increases immediately.

The component parts involved in a blown capacitor are the failed capacitor cell itself, the degraded dielectric fluid that can no longer sustain the electric field, and the compromised aluminum casing that may bulge or leak as internal pressure rises. A failed capacitor is a distinct fault from a failed contactor or a refrigerant leak, though all three can produce similar symptoms of an AC system not cooling. Proper AC capacitor failure diagnosis isolates the capacitor as the cause before any other component is replaced.

An air conditioning contractor with electrical diagnostic training is the right resource for this repair. Capacitors store a lethal charge even after power is disconnected; discharge procedures are mandatory before any hands-on testing.

Capacitor function versus capacitor failure

Capacitor function. The contrast state to capacitor failure. Means the component holds its rated capacitance within ±6%, operates below its voltage ceiling, and delivers consistent start and run run torque to the motor on every cycle. System normal operation depends on this: the compressor starts within two to three seconds, the condenser fan reaches full speed, and the refrigerant circuit builds to operating pressure without the motor drawing excess current.

A capacitor in good condition reads at or near its labeled µF value on a calibrated meter. It shows no physical deformation, no oil residue on the casing, and no discoloration at the terminals. Tolerance matters. Replacing a capacitor before it drifts outside the ±6% tolerance band. Typically as part of a seasonal tune-up. Keeps the system in normal operation and avoids the cascade of motor stress that a blown capacitor triggers.
