An electric furnace, called an electric air handler or resistance furnace, is a forced-air heating unit that warms air by passing it across electrically energized heating elements. ### What an electric furnace is

An electric furnace is a resistance-based heating unit. It converts electrical energy directly into heat with no combustion byproducts. Air moves across a set of electric heating elements, absorbs that heat, and a blower motor pushes the warmed air through the duct system into each room. No gas line or flue is required. That makes installation straightforward in homes served only by electrical utilities. The equipment connects to a standard 240V single-phase circuit, the same supply voltage used by electric dryers and water heaters.

Three core components make up the unit: electric heating elements, a sequencer relay, and a blower motor. The sequencer relay staggers element activation so the system does not draw its full load at once, protecting the electrical panel from a sudden spike. Licensed technicians at AC Plus Heating & Air diagnose faults at each of these components during a service call. The unit is not treated as a single assembly.

### Measurable attributes: capacity, voltage, and efficiency

Electric furnace heating capacity ranges from 5 kW to 25 kW. The right size depends on square footage, insulation level, and window area. A unit sized too small runs continuously without reaching the set temperature; one sized too large short-cycles and wears components faster. Voltage matters. Virtually every residential model on the market today requires 240V single-phase power.

Efficiency is where the electric furnace stands apart from gas equipment. The Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency (AFUE) rating for a resistance furnace runs from 95% to 100%, because nearly all electrical energy converts to usable heat with minimal loss. A [heat pump](https://ac-plus-heating-air.mintlify.site/heat-pump) moves heat rather than generating it, so its effective efficiency can exceed 100% in mild climates, but a resistance furnace holds a clear advantage over older gas models that vent a portion of combustion heat outdoors. Victorville's mild winters make the operating economics of an electric furnace reasonable for many households.

### How an electric furnace fits into the broader HVAC system

An electric furnace is one component inside a larger forced-air system. That system includes a thermostat, ductwork, supply registers, and return grilles. In a split-system configuration, the furnace or air handler sits indoors and pairs with an outdoor condensing unit that handles cooling in summer. The outdoor unit connects to the same duct network. This means the blower motor inside the electric air handler moves conditioned air year-round, not only during heating season.

Homes with a [central air conditioning system](https://ac-plus-heating-air.mintlify.site/central-air-conditioning-system) already in place often use the existing air handler as the heating source by adding electric heating elements to the cabinet. This avoids running a second set of ducts. Homes without existing ductwork have an alternative in a [ductless mini split system](https://ac-plus-heating-air.mintlify.site/ductless-mini-split-system), which delivers both heating and cooling without any duct infrastructure. An [air conditioning contractor](https://ac-plus-heating-air.mintlify.site/air-conditioning-contractor) evaluates which configuration fits the home before recommending equipment.

### Electric furnace vs. Gas furnace and heat pump

A gas furnace burns natural gas or propane inside a heat exchanger, then vents combustion gases through a flue to the outside. That combustion process introduces carbon monoxide risk. It requires annual inspection of the heat exchanger for cracks. An electric furnace carries none of those risks because there is no flame, no flue, and no combustion gas. The tradeoff is operating cost: electricity rates per BTU of heat delivered are typically higher than natural gas rates in most U.S. Markets, though Victorville households without gas service have no practical alternative in a ducted system.

A heat pump differs from both by moving heat from outdoor air into the home rather than generating it. Above roughly 35°F, efficiency is higher. A heat pump delivers more heat per kilowatt-hour than a resistance furnace at those temperatures. Below that threshold, heat pump output drops and many systems switch to electric resistance backup strips, which are functionally identical to the elements inside a standalone resistance furnace. Understanding this distinction helps homeowners choose the right equipment for the High Desert climate, where winter nights can dip into the low 30s.

### Common problems and signs an electric furnace needs repair

Electric furnace problems fall into three categories: element failure, sequencer failure, and blower motor failure. A failed heating element produces weak or uneven heat. Some rooms stay cold while others reach temperature. A faulty sequencer relay can cause all elements to attempt energizing simultaneously, potentially tripping the circuit breaker. Blower motor failure stops airflow entirely, so the elements overheat and the high-limit safety switch shuts the unit down.

Four signs indicate the unit needs professional attention:

- **No heat output**: No heat output with the blower running points to a failed element or open sequencer.

- **Tripped breaker**: A breaker that trips on startup signals a sequencer fault or a short in the element circuit.

- **Burning smell**: A burning smell on first use of the season is often dust burning off elements, but a persistent smell warrants inspection.

- **Weak airflow**: Weak airflow with normal heat output points to a blower motor or capacitor issue rather than an element problem.

### Electric furnace installation and replacement in Victorville

Electric furnace installation requires matching the unit's kilowatt rating to the home's heat load, confirming the electrical panel has capacity for a 240V dedicated circuit, and verifying that existing ductwork is sized for the blower's airflow. Ductwork matters more than most homeowners expect. Undersized ductwork creates static pressure that shortens blower motor life and reduces comfort. A proper load calculation, not a rule-of-thumb square-footage estimate, determines the correct capacity.

Electric furnace replacement service follows the same sizing process, with the added step of inspecting the existing duct system for leaks or damage before the new unit goes in. Duct leaks are not a minor issue. Sealing them at the time of replacement recovers heat that might otherwise escape into unconditioned attic or crawl space, improving the effective efficiency of the new equipment.
